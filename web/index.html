Pain & Politics

Exploring the correlation between county-level distress metrics and Trump voting patterns

Setting the Context

Research has shown correlations between place-level distress—including overdose rates, "deaths of despair," and stalled life expectancy—and support for Trump in 2016 and 2020.

This analysis explores these patterns at the county level using exploratory spatial data analysis (ESDA).

Mapping Physical Distress

The CDC PLACES dataset provides modeled estimates of the percentage of adults reporting frequent physical distress (≥14 days of poor physical health per month).

Notice the geographic clustering in Appalachia, the rural South, and parts of the Southwest.

Trump Vote Share 2016

Trump's 2016 electoral success was particularly strong in rural counties and regions experiencing economic transition.

Compare this pattern with the physical distress map—do you see overlaps?

Bivariate Relationship

Using bivariate LISA analysis, we can identify statistically significant spatial clusters where high pain and high Trump support co-locate.

  • Red (HH): High distress, high Trump support
  • Blue (LL): Low distress, low Trump support
  • Pink (HL): High distress, low Trump support
  • Light Blue (LH): Low distress, high Trump support

The Overdose Crisis

Drug overdose mortality rates provide another lens on community distress.

Counties with the highest overdose rates often—but not always—overlap with Trump strongholds.

Hot Spot Analysis

Getis-Ord Gi* analysis identifies statistically significant spatial clusters of high (hot spots) and low (cold spots) values.

Hot spots of physical distress concentrate in specific regions, revealing geographic patterns of health disparities.

Model Results

After controlling for rurality, education, income, and demographics, the association between pain metrics and Trump support remains statistically significant but modest.

Spatial models reveal strong geographic clustering, suggesting that local context matters.

Important Caveats

  • Ecological Fallacy: County-level patterns don't imply individual behavior
  • Association ≠ Causation: These are correlations, not causal relationships
  • Data Limitations: Some estimates are modeled; small counties may be suppressed
  • Complex Factors: Many unmeasured variables influence voting patterns
